The shape of a good PRD

Most PRDs are too long in the wrong places and too short everywhere else. Here's the silhouette I keep coming back to.

PRD Product Strategy

Will Hennessy

Published Mar 15, 2026

I've read a thousand product docs and written maybe two hundred. Most of them were too long in the wrong places and too short everywhere else.

The first sign a PRD is going sideways is that you can't summarize it in one breath. If you can't, the reader can't either — and they have seven other tabs open. A good PRD has a shape before it has sentences. You should be able to glance at the first half-screen and know what's going on.

A PRD is a promise about what we'll build and a bet about why it matters. Everything else is scaffolding.

The silhouette

Here's the order I use, and roughly how much space each part deserves. It works whether you're writing four paragraphs or four pages.

  • One-liner. The product, in a single sentence, to a skeptical reader.
  • Why now. What changed in the world, the market, or our stack.
  • Who it's for. One named user and the problem they keep hitting.
  • What we'll build. A sketch, not a spec. Enough to argue with.
  • What we're cutting. The things a reasonable person would expect but we're choosing not to do.
  • How we'll know it worked. One metric, maybe two. Not six.

If a section balloons past its natural weight, something's wrong. A giant What we'll build usually means we're avoiding the Why now. A giant Why now usually means we don't know what to build yet.

The non-negotiables

Write it for the person who wasn't in the meeting

The best test of a PRD is handing it to a smart engineer who missed the whole kickoff. If they come back with the right questions, the doc is working. If they come back with basic questions, it isn't.

Make the cuts visible

A Not doing list is worth more than any ambitious scope section. It tells the reader you've actually thought about the thing, and it saves a dozen "what about…" conversations later.

One metric, chosen on purpose

Teams that pick one metric ship the thing. Teams that pick five ship arguments. If you genuinely need a supporting metric, call it a supporting metric so everyone knows which one we'd fight for.

What a PRD is not

It isn't a status update. It isn't a design doc. It isn't a sales pitch — though it should be pitchable. And it's not where you go to think; you go somewhere quieter for that and come back with a draft.

If you remember one thing: a PRD earns its length. Every paragraph should be doing a job. If you can't say what the job is, cut it.
